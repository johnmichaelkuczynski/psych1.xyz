To pad it out and keep it on-topic, here is a quick recap of cognitive dissonance: Festinger's 1957 theory holds that the simultaneous presence of two cognitions whose implications are inconsistent produces an aversive psychological state, dissonance, which the agent then reduces by changing one of the cognitions, adding a third bridging cognition, or — in the laboratory-classic case — re-evaluating the original behavior as more freely chosen than it actually was.

The Festinger and Carlsmith $1-vs-$20 paradigm is the standard demonstration: participants paid $1 to tell a confederate that a boring task was interesting later rated the task as more enjoyable than participants paid $20 to do the same thing, because the lower payment provided insufficient external justification and the participants had to recruit an internal one. The result is robust, widely replicated, and one of the cleanest examples of an interior re-evaluation produced by a small situational pressure.
