The Stanford Prison Experiment is usually summarized as a demonstration that ordinary people will become abusive once you put them in a prison-guard role. That summary is wrong in instructive ways. Philip Zimbardo's 1971 study did show striking and rapid role-uptake by college-aged men assigned to play guards in a mock prison built in the basement of Stanford's psychology building, but the design was not the clean, hands-off experiment its popular telling suggests. Zimbardo himself acted as superintendent, briefed the guards before the study began, and only halted the experiment six days in because a graduate student visiting the basement was visibly shocked at what she saw.

What the experiment is good evidence for is the speed with which a constructed situation can shape behavior. Within forty-eight hours, prisoners were behaving like prisoners — sullen, defeated, occasionally rebellious — and guards were behaving like guards, with a clear escalation in punitive creativity over the course of the week. The role labels themselves were doing work that the participants had not consciously chosen; this is the "situational" reading favored by Zimbardo and by later social psychologists like Lee Ross and Richard Nisbett, who built the broader "fundamental attribution error" framework on top of related evidence. The dispositional alternative — that the abusive guards were already cruel people, mis-selected by the screening — does not fit the data; the participants were psychologically screened for normalcy in advance.

But the study is weaker evidence than its reputation suggests for the stronger claim that any of us, in those roles, would have done the same. Recent reanalyses by Texier (2018) and Le Texier's full archival reconstruction show that several of the most-cited guard cruelties were prompted directly by Zimbardo's team. The Banuazizi-Movahedi critique from 1975 anticipated this: the experiment may be measuring compliance with a confederate authority more than spontaneous role-uptake.

The pedagogical implication is that situational explanations are powerful and undersold in everyday reasoning — we routinely under-weight context when we judge other people's behavior, especially their failures. The methodological implication is that demand characteristics scale with how much the experimenter participates in the setup; an unbriefed control condition is the obvious missing piece. Together they make Stanford a better case for skepticism about overconfident interpretation of single studies than it is for any clean theory of evil-by-circumstance.
